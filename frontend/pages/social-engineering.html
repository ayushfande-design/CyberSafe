All About Social Engineering

Social engineering is the use of psychological manipulation to trick people into divulging confidential information, giving up valuables, or granting access to computer systems. Rather than relying on technical hacking methods, social engineering exploits human psychology, such as trust, urgency, and fear, to bypass security measures. Attacks can be conducted in person, over the phone, through email, or on social media.

Common Social Engineering Attack Techniques

  • Phishing: This is one of the most common forms of social engineering, where attackers send fraudulent communications—often email, but also text messages (smishing) or phone calls (vishing)—that appear to come from a legitimate or trusted source. The goal is to trick victims into revealing sensitive information or to install malware.
  • Pretexting: This involves creating a fabricated scenario (a pretext) to obtain information or access. The attacker might impersonate a bank official, IT support staff, or another authority figure to gain trust.
  • Baiting: Similar to a real-world Trojan horse, baiting uses a false promise to entice a victim. A common tactic involves leaving a malware-infected USB drive in a public place with a tempting label like "Confidential Payroll".
  • Tailgating (or Piggybacking): This is a physical security breach where an unauthorized person follows an authorized employee into a restricted area, often relying on social courtesy.
  • Quid Pro Quo: This translates to "a favor for a favor." In this scam, an attacker offers a service or reward in exchange for information, like fake IT support asking for login credentials.
  • Scareware: Attackers use fear to manipulate victims into taking action, often appearing as a pop-up ad that claims the user's computer is infected with a virus.
  • Watering Hole Attack: This targets a specific group of users by infecting a website that they are known to frequent, waiting for them to visit and become infected with malware.

How to Protect Against Social Engineering Attacks

  • Think Before You Act: If a message creates a sense of urgency or an emotional appeal, slow down and carefully consider the request.
  • Verify the Source: Independently confirm the sender of any suspicious communication, especially if they are asking for personal information.
  • Use Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA): Enabling MFA adds an extra layer of security to your accounts.
  • Update and Use Security Software: Keep your antivirus, anti-malware, and other security software up-to-date.
  • Limit Your Online Footprint: Oversharing personal details on social media can provide attackers with information for a more convincing attack.
  • Be Wary of Offers That Seem Too Good to Be True: If an offer for a prize or reward seems too generous, it is likely a scam.
